Conference paper: How did the Pratt & Whitney J58 (JT11D-20) Turbojet Engines work to enable the SR-71 Blackbird to travel at speeds of over Mach 3?

Conference project

Abstract

In this project, I look at five of the different design features used by this Pratt & Whitney engine that I believe are key to its success. I also provide brief explanations of how turbojet, ramjet and scramjet engines work before going on to talk about which elements from these engines were applied to the SR-71. Using data from various sources alongside the aircraft’s flight manual, I was able to understand how each of these systems function and how they contributed to the performance of the engine at supersonic speeds. I found that special bleed air bypass valves alongside a very unique inlet design were some of the biggest contributors, with these elements producing significantly more power than the actual turbine at supersonic speeds. While I found the bypass valves to be the most significant feature, I concluded that all of these elements required each other for the engine to function properly and so they were all vastly important to the operation of the aircraft.

Introduction

This project will discuss the key features that enabled the SR-71 to sustain supersonic speeds for hours at a time. Although many elements contributed to this, I will be focusing primarily on the engine and intakes. I will first focus on how different engines work, allowing for a better understanding of the specifics I talk about later. Then, having looked at some base statistics for the engine, I will examine certain elements of the engine which are crucial to its supersonic capabilities including the bypass ducts, intakes and fuel. This will give an insight as to how the SR-71 could sustain supersonic flight for extended periods (Pace, 2016).

Analysis and discussion

To understand the concepts of the SR-71’s engines, it is important to first have an idea of how three of the base engine types function.

Starting with the turbojet engine, this draws air in at the front using stages of fans which compress the air, making it denser and denser (Hall, 2021a). This dense air is then combusted causing it to expand rapidly, before getting expelled out from exhaust nozzle at a very high pressure and speed relative to when it entered the engine (Derbel and Beneda, 2025). Since it has fans which compress the air, it can run when stationary, drawing in air to compress (Hall, 2021b).

A ramjet works in the same way except it doesn’t have fans to compress the air, using a diffuser instead. Because of this, these engines only start to work at speeds over Mach 1 as the diffuser requires air ramming into it, allowing it to slow the air down to subsonic speeds before compressing it, thereby creating the high pressures needed for combustion (Gerbis, 2024).

Finally, a scramjet works similarly to a ramjet, but instead of slowing down the airflow to subsonic speeds, it compresses and combusts it while it is still at supersonic speed (Hall, 2021c). This greatly reduces the drag as there is less air resistance slowing the aircraft down.

The SR-71 used two afterburning, axial-flow, continuous-bleed Pratt & Whitney J58 (JT11D-20) Turbojet Engines (Pace, 2016) with each engine producing 34,000 pounds of thrust (Admin, 2017) with afterburner (at sea level in a static condition). The aircraft weighed roughly 140,000 pounds at take-off (NASA, 2008) giving a thrust-to-weight ratio of 1:0.486 which improved throughout the flight, as fuel was used, ending each mission with a thrust ratio of nearly 1:1. The turbofan part of the engine had 9 fans which would sequentially compress the air to 8.8 times the intake pressure (airandspace.si.edu, n.d.) before it was combusted in the core.

One feature that made this engine so special were the bleed valves that ran from the intake to the afterburner, bypassing some compressor stages. These valves would open at around Mach 2 (see Figure 1), channelling air directly into the fuel spray rings in the afterburner (SENIOR CROWN Program Security Classification Guide, 1986). This transition reduced the airflow into the turbine from 80% to just 8% as valves channelled more and more to the afterburner, effectively converting the engine into a ramjet (Admin, 2017). With most of the air now bypassing the compressor, the engineers needed to find different ways to compress the air. This was achieved with a unique inlet design.

At the front of each inlet is a spike that moved forwards and backwards, altering the amount of airflow and, at supersonic speeds, the shape of the shockwave. For engine start-up and all speeds under Mach 1.6, the spike was pushed fully forward. However, at higher speeds, the spike would pull back 1.625 inches per 0.1 increment increase in Mach number (D’Urso, 2019) as seen in Figure 1. By reducing the size of the throat, the air is channelled through, this simultaneously increased the volume and the density of air channelled through the inlet, providing the engine with more oxygen which enabled it to burn more fuel and ultimately produce more thrust.

Two key parts of the exhaust nozzle at the rear of the engine were also designed to improve the function of the engine. The ejector flaps altered the volume of the exhaust nozzle allowing large changes in the speed and density of the air traveling through the engine. At sub-sonic speeds, the flaps were closed, decreasing the volume of the exhaust nozzle and forcing the air out of a smaller gap and therefore at a higher speed. At this speed, the tertiary doors between the compressor and exhaust opened adding more air into the exhaust nozzle, increasing the pressure further. As the aircraft approached Mach 1.5, the tertiary doors closed and the exhaust nozzle widened as the pressure and speed of the airflow increases. This continued until the aircraft hit its top speed of about Mach 3.2 where the output of the exhaust nozzle was wider than the input (SENIOR CROWN Program Security Classification Guide, 1986).

Since the engines operated under a unique set of parameters, a special fuel, JP-7 was developed (Admin, 2017). The aircraft needed to be as light as possible therefore instead of using separate cooling systems and coolant, they used the fuel to draw heat away from various components related to and around the engines. This required a fuel that wouldn’t break down under such temperatures and contained enough lubricant to ensure the components ran smoothly. Additionally, the fuel burned with a very bright flame, reducing the heat transferred to parts in the afterburner. At full power, the two engines would use over 50,000 gallons of this fuel per hour (Davis, 2017).

In conclusion, there are lots of key elements which allow this engine to function as it does. In my opinion, the most crucial is the bypass system that converts this engine into a ramjet as that provides the power and efficiency to sustain Mach 3 flight. That being said, the engine wouldn’t function as successfully as it does if any of these design elements were removed.
